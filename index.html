Round 1 (Monday) — likely questions, your answers

Conversational. Don't recite. These are the shape of the answer, in your voice. Land the bolded number.

Walk me through your background.
I'm a last-mile operator and a builder. I started in rail at BNSF running terminals and a 673-person team. Then Amazon, where I built two programs from scratch, including the launch-execution program for their autonomous robot delivery stations. Now I'm a director at Walmart over third-party delivery technology, running fleet integrations and the AI tooling behind 100,000+ deliveries a day. The thread through all of it is taking something new and making it run reliably at scale.
Why SoftBank Robotics, why now?
Two threads in my career, robotics and last-mile operations, have always been separate jobs. Here they're the same job. You're taking robots into the real world and making them deliver, which is exactly what I did with Amazon's robot stations and what I do with fleets at Walmart now. The remote setup also fits where I am in my life right now, and what I've heard about the team and the pace pulled me in.
What do you know about what we're building?
You're the largest autonomous robot orchestrator, you've proven you can deploy and run fleets of robots at scale and keep them up through SBR Connect. What excites me is where this team is taking that, direct-to-consumer last-mile, getting a delivery to someone's door with ground robots. That's the exact intersection I've lived, I built Amazon's robot delivery stations and I run third-party last-mile to the customer at Walmart. SoftBank's own framing nails it, the challenge isn't the technology, it's operationalizing it for measurable outcomes, and getting a robot to reliably finish that last hundred feet to the door is pure ops.
Tell me about something you built from zero.
At Amazon I built the launch-execution program for their autonomous robot delivery stations. There was no playbook. I'd take an empty building and a robot platform and stand up the whole operation, coordinating the construction, the process, the bill of materials, the secure delivery network (the S-DEN) install, the timelines, testing, then the ramp. The first launches were rough, we were learning what broke at scale in real time. So I studied the standard conveyor-station launch, worked backwards, and built the playbook to standardize the next ones off the real failures. We cut ramp defects 17%, and it became the standard the other launches used.
How do you think about fleet uptime and reliability at scale?
My rule is the team should know a robot is failing before the customer does. At Walmart that meant exception alerting on top of a performance framework, and that's what lifted on-time 15% and cut cancellations 35%. For a delivery-robot fleet I'd watch uptime, mean-time-between-failures, cost-per-delivery, and delivery success rate, and use the fleet-management layer, your SBR Connect stack, so a degraded robot pulls itself out of rotation instead of failing a delivery at someone's door. The vehicle changes; the reliability and unit-economics discipline doesn't.
Biggest scaling challenge you've solved?
We were onboarding delivery carriers in quarters. I rebuilt the process and got it to weeks at a 99% on-time launch rate across ten platforms. The unlock was standardizing the playbook so each new launch stopped being a custom project.
Tell me about a fleet-management system you've built. (your closest analog to this role, lead with it)
This is the closest thing I've done to your world. At Walmart I built our van delivery-management system, the TMS and the app, and I built it twice. First from scratch, then priorities shifted to stabilizing our gig-carrier network, so I took my business requirements and rebuilt it on the 3P carrier's off-the-shelf platform instead. That became the app and TMS/DMS we used to scale from a small pilot to roughly eighteen hundred vehicles in a year. Early on our internal and external systems couldn't talk, so I stood up a team to manually coordinate across our vendors, their sub-contractors, our internal team, and field ops, which worked but meant headcount had to grow with the fleet. So I worked with the carrier to automate the whole thing: it standardized the communication, killed the manual issue-tracking, only alerted us to true exceptions, and pushed alerts to the sub-contractors so they could manage their own operations. That broke the headcount-grows-with-fleet curve. It's exactly the muscle a robot delivery fleet needs.
Tell me about building or right-sizing a team.
My favorite version is from BNSF. During a coal downturn they handed me a terminal that had been run by a superintendent, a terminal manager, four front-line trainmasters, and a division trainmaster, and I ran it alone, building the process and systems to operate with just the hourly yardmasters. When the volume came back I didn't just rehire the old structure, I right-sized it and built the team to a leaner shape, a terminal manager and three trainmasters, leaning on a neighboring yard for surge. I've run both ends of that cycle, stripping an operation down to run lean and building it back deliberately. That's the muscle a brand-new team needs.
How do you work across engineering, operations, and partners?
What keeps it from falling apart is a single source of truth and clear ownership, plus I do enough of the technical work myself, SQL, the AI tooling, that engineers treat me as a peer instead of a layer to route around. At Walmart I'm the connector across engineering, store ops, finance, and the carrier partners, and the reason it holds is that I'm in the actual data with them, not just running the meeting.
Tell me about a failure, or something that surprised you.
Early ARDS launches, I over-indexed on the plan and under-indexed on what actually broke on the floor. We hit ramp defects I didn't see coming because I trusted the model over the operators. Here's what surprised me: the old Mech-Lite stations could hit timelines by adding labor or paralleling builds, but with the robotics the timelines had to be exact. The network couldn't come up until the S-DEN, our secure delivery externalized network, was installed, and the autonomous robots couldn't run until the site was mapped. The fix was to build the playbook from the floor up. It's why I now start every launch by talking to the people doing the work, mapping the real dependencies and limits, and building in buffers where the schedule compresses.
Remote, and start timing?
Remote works well for me, I run distributed teams now and I build a lot of async tooling. On start timing I'd want to land on a date that's fair to the team and to me, and I've already thought through a couple of options that could work.
What are you looking for in your next role?
A place where standing things up from zero is the job, not a side project, and where I'm close enough to both the ops and the code to actually move a reliability number. This role is exactly that. The building-from-nothing is the part I want more of, and here it's the whole point.

Ops / technical Q&A (likely with him)

"How do you scale ops without scaling headcount?" → My favorite problem. So we automated it: standardized the comms, killed manual issue-tracking, alerted only on true exceptions, and pushed those alerts to the sub-contractors to self-manage. That broke the headcount-grows-with-fleet curve. For a robot fleet it's the same lever — how many robots one operator can safely run, and how you push that ratio with better exception routing.
"What's hard about scaling sidewalk delivery robots?" → It's not the demo, it's the unit economics and the long tail. Cost per delivery and the intervention rate are the real battle — every teleop takeover is a human you're paying. Then merchant density, weather, sidewalk and permit rules, uptime. The integrator's job is making someone else's robot perform predictably at scale, which is exactly what I do.
"First 90 days?" → Learn the current scoreboard — deliveries/robot/day, CPD, intervention rate, uptime — find the one metric throttling growth, instrument it.
