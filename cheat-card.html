Your proof stories
4
Tell me about a fleet-management system you've built. ★ lead with this
TALK TRACKThis is the closest thing I've done to your world. I built our van delivery-management system. But here's the part that matters: I didn't build it from scratch. Priorities shifted to stabilizing our third-party network, so I took my requirements and built on a carrier's off-the-shelf platform instead. That became the app and TMS/DMS we used to scale the fleet from a small pilot to roughly eighteen hundred in a year. Early on, our systems and theirs couldn't talk. So I ran a team coordinating it all by hand, across our vendors, their sub-contractors, our team, and field ops. It worked. But headcount grew with the fleet. So I worked with the carrier to automate it. Standardized the comms. Killed the manual tracking. Alert only on true exceptions. Pushed the rest to the sub-contractors to manage themselves. That broke the headcount-grows-with-fleet curve.
HIT THESE
  • Pivoted from in-house to building on a third-party carrier's platform (the integrator move).
  • Scaled the fleet from a small pilot to ~1,800 vans in a year.
  • Manual coordination worked but headcount grew with the fleet.
  • Automated: standardized comms, killed manual tracking, alert only on true exceptions, sub-contractors self-manage.
  • Result: broke the headcount-grows-with-fleet curve. = SBR Connect + integrator + exception-based ops.

6
How do you think about fleet uptime / reliability at scale?
TALK TRACKMy rule is simple. The team should know a robot is failing before the customer does. At Walmart that was exception alerting on top of a performance framework. It lifted on-time fifteen percent and cut cancellations thirty-five. For a robot fleet I'd watch the same shape. Uptime, mean-time-between-failures, delivery success, cost-per-delivery. And I'd wire it so a degraded robot pulls itself out of rotation instead of failing at someone's door. Reliability is a process game, not a heroics game.
HIT THESE
  • Rule: know a robot is failing before the customer does.
  • Exception alerting + performance framework → on-time +15%, cancellations −35%.
  • Robot metrics: uptime, MTBF, delivery success rate, cost-per-delivery.
  • A degraded robot pulls itself out of rotation instead of failing at the door.
  • Use SBR Connect as the layer that surfaces the true exceptions.

Build your own cheat card (steal this)

One structure anyone can copy. The idea: don't memorize answers, prep a talk track you can talk from.

1
Write a 60-second opening — your background as a story with a through-line, ending on why this company. It's "tell me about yourself," and you'll reuse pieces of it all interview.
2
Pick 3 power stories that fit this job, one real number each. Pin them on top and bend answers back to one.
3
Find their one line — read the company's own words, pull a phrase that's also true of you, and mirror it back.
4
Per question: a TALK TRACK in your own voice + 4-6 "HIT THESE" points + the number. Write how you actually talk, short sentences, then read the points to stay on track.
5
STAR, but short: Situation → Task → Action (the bulk) → Result (the number). Then say it out loud once.
